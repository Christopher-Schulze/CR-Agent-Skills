# Score Anchors Reference

Use these anchors when judging concepts, rendered implementations, or close design calls. Score 1-5. A 4 is implementation-ready. A 5 is world-class. Critical dimensions below 4 require repair, regeneration, or explicit user-approved deviation.

## Subject Fit

- 1: Could fit almost any unrelated product after swapping copy.
- 2: Uses a few domain words or icons, but the visual thesis is generic.
- 3: Contains recognizable domain signals, yet the layout and components are template-like.
- 4: Product artifacts, workflow, data, tone, and visual system clearly belong to this use case.
- 5: The interface feels inevitable for this exact product; no other category could reuse it without redesign.

## First Viewport Thesis

- 1: User cannot tell what it is or what to do first.
- 2: Product type is visible, but priority, action, or result state is confused.
- 3: Clear enough, but focal point, copy, or action competes with secondary material.
- 4: Product, priority, action, and state/result are understood within five seconds.
- 5: The opening view is a memorable thesis: product world, value proof, action, and continuation are all obvious and elegant.

## Copy And Content Realism

- 1: Filler slogans, fake metrics, unreadable text, or unsafe claims drive the UI.
- 2: Some real labels exist, but copy is vague, repetitive, or generic.
- 3: Plausible copy with occasional filler, weak empty/error states, or loose domain data.
- 4: Copy, labels, sample data, claims, states, and actions are specific, concise, plausible, and user-side.
- 5: Content feels production-authored: every string improves orientation, trust, decision, recovery, or action.

## Density And Text-Space Balance

- 1: Stuffed, chaotic, or nearly empty with no craft.
- 2: More than 3-4 competing priorities, text walls, or decorative gaps.
- 3: Acceptable balance, but a few sections feel wordy, repetitive, or hollow.
- 4: Density posture matches the job; text, data, media, controls, and quiet space are intentionally proportioned.
- 5: Rich but calm. Every viewport has one clear focal priority, useful breathing room, and no weak filler.

## Component Craft

- 1: Browser defaults, raw library primitives, placeholder boxes, or basic cards.
- 2: Light styling, inconsistent padding/radii, weak icons, missing states, or generic SaaS surfaces.
- 3: Mostly styled, but one or more repeated components lower the perceived quality.
- 4: Each visible component family has deliberate type, spacing, surface, icon treatment, states, and responsive behavior.
- 5: Even small controls feel custom-finished, coherent, tactile, accessible, and worthy of the overall design.

## Typography

- 1: Browser/default sizing, weak hierarchy, awkward wraps, or unreadable text.
- 2: Basic scale with little separation between display, body, labels, controls, and data.
- 3: Clean hierarchy, but type personality or control typography is interchangeable.
- 4: Display, body, UI/control, label, data, and mobile roles are deliberate and readable.
- 5: Typography carries identity and precision; line breaks, rhythm, numeric/data treatment, and control text all feel designed.

## Spatial Composition

- 1: Random placement, poor alignment, cramped edges, or no focal hierarchy.
- 2: Grid exists, but whitespace, scale, rhythm, or section transitions feel accidental.
- 3: Competent layout with some repetitive blocks, weak counterweight, or unresolved mobile spacing.
- 4: Focal hierarchy, optical alignment, breathing room, edge discipline, and responsive rhythm are deliberate.
- 5: Composition feels editorially authored; scale, negative space, asymmetry, and sequencing make the product easier to understand.

## Asset Fit

- 1: Missing, broken, stock-like, blurry, cropped badly, or unrelated assets.
- 2: Assets exist but clash in lighting, crop, color temperature, icon style, or surrounding spacing.
- 3: Usable assets with some mismatch or weak implementation readiness.
- 4: Images, icons, logos/marks, cutouts, backgrounds, or sprites are coherent, high-quality, and implementation-ready.
- 5: Assets are signature-grade: they reveal the product world and blend perfectly with surfaces, type, motion, and interaction.

## Interaction Realism

- 1: Static facade, inert controls, or impossible state model.
- 2: A few clickable-looking areas, but states, feedback, or primary path are unclear.
- 3: Primary path is plausible, but secondary states, errors, loading, or mobile behavior are thin.
- 4: Selected, hover, focus, loading, empty, error, success, disabled, confirmation, and mobile states are inferable where relevant.
- 5: Interaction feels like a real product: state changes, recovery, feedback, and motion all clarify user progress.

## Implementation Feasibility

- 1: Requires static screenshots, impossible assets, hidden overflow hacks, or non-repo stack changes.
- 2: Buildable only with fragile one-offs, hardcoded dimensions, or duplicated component families.
- 3: Buildable, but needs simplification or a few justified fidelity exceptions.
- 4: Clean repo-native implementation path: tokens, primitives, variants, assets, responsive layout, and verification are clear.
- 5: The concept naturally decomposes into maintainable components while preserving a high-fidelity agency-grade finish.
